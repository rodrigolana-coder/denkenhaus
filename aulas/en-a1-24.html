How to talk about your daily routine

A: What time do you get up? — B: I get up at seven o’clock.

A: What time does school start? — B: It starts at eight o’clock.

A: What time do you go to bed? — B: At half past ten.

A: What time do you usually have dinner? — B: Around seven o’clock.

Grammar

1. Asking about routines

What time do you get up?

What time do you leave home?

What time do you have lunch?

What time do you go to bed?

With he / she / it

What time does he get up?

What time does she start work?

What time does the museum open?

2. Saying the time — Digital time

Say the hour, then the minutes.

8:00 → It’s eight o’clock.

8:05 → It’s eight oh five.

8:10 → It’s eight ten.

8:15 → It’s eight fifteen.

8:24 → It’s eight twenty-four.

8:30 → It’s eight thirty.

8:40 → It’s eight forty.

8:45 → It’s eight forty-five.

8:55 → It’s eight fifty-five.

3. Saying the time — Traditional time

These expressions are also very common, especially in British English.

8:15 → It’s quarter past eight.

8:30 → It’s half past eight.

8:45 → It’s quarter to nine.

8:25 → It’s twenty-five past eight.

8:35 → It’s twenty-five to nine.

4. Tip

Both forms are correct.

8:24 → It’s eight twenty-four.

8:30 → It’s eight thirty. / It’s half past eight.

8:45 → It’s eight forty-five. / It’s quarter to nine.

In everyday English, especially in American English, people often prefer the digital format. In British English, both forms are very common.

5. Review — Present Simple

We use the Present Simple to talk about routines.

I get up at seven.

She starts work at eight.

He has lunch at noon.

We go to bed at eleven.

6. Review — Adverbs of frequency

always

usually

often

sometimes

never

I usually have breakfast at seven.

She always arrives early.

We sometimes eat at a café.

He never goes to bed late.
